# Understanding 5.3 Biometrics
Status of this document: 

⚠️ Draft mode.

## EN 301 549 v4.1.0 draft
Normative requirement (EN 301 549 v4.1.0 draft)
> “Where ICT uses biometric characteristics, the ICT shall not rely on the use of a biometric characteristic as the only means of user identification or control of the ICT.
> NOTE: Some examples of biometric characteristics are fingerprints, eye retinal patterns, voice, and face.” 


## Intent
Not all users are able to provide or reliably use biometric characteristics for identification or interaction with ICT systems. Biometric systems may also fail under environmental, technical, or personal conditions.

This requirement ensures that **biometric characteristics are not the sole means of user identification or control of ICT systems**.

**At least one non-biometric alternative method is required to be available to users.**

Biometric systems can create **barriers to access** when:

- A user cannot provide the required biometric input (e.g., missing fingerprint, facial differences, speech impairment).
- Environmental conditions prevent reliable capture (e.g., low lighting, background noise, gloves, masks).
- Technical limitations cause recognition failures (e.g., sensor accuracy issues or bias in biometric systems).
- Temporary conditions (injury, illness, voice loss) prevent use of the biometric method.
- Users are excluded due to variability in biometric traits across populations or algorithmic bias (e.g., face recognition bias) in biometric systems.

This requirement ensures that **access to ICT systems remains available when biometric methods are unavailable or unsuitable**.


### This requirement benefits
This requirement benefits:

- Users with permanent disabilities affecting biometric capture (e.g., limb differences affecting fingerprint recognition, speech differences affecting voice recognition).
- Users with temporary impairments (e.g., injury, surgery, illness, voice loss).
- Older adults whose biometric characteristics may change over time.
- Users in environments where biometric capture is unreliable (e.g., low-light conditions, noise, wet or dry hands, gloves, masks, or carrying objects).
- Users who choose not to use biometric authentication for privacy or personal reasons.


### Examples
If a user has previously enrolled in biometric identification and later experiences issues with those biometric characteristics (e.g., damaged fingerprint or changes in facial appearance affecting recognition), **the user can use a non-biometric method to complete login or control functions**.

- A smartphone that supports Face ID or fingerprint authentication also supports PIN, password, or passcode authentication.
- A banking application using voice recognition also supports authentication methods that do not rely on voice input, such as password and multi-factor authentication.
- A workplace access system using iris scanning also provides alternatives such as access cards (badges) or PIN-based authentication or physical tokens (passcode, secure tokens).
- A voice-controlled smart device also provides alternative input methods such as physical buttons or remote control.


## Evaluation methods
If ICT functionality uses biometric characteristics, verify that:

- A non-biometric alternative method exists for all functions requiring biometric input.
- The alternative method does not require prior biometric enrollment.
- The alternative supports all authentication and control functions available via biometric methods without restriction.
- The alternative is available in all contexts where the biometric method is available.


### Failures
The following are considered failures:

- The ICT relies solely on biometric characteristics for authentication or control.
- No non-biometric alternative method is available.
- The alternative method requires prior biometric enrollment.
- The alternative method is only available after a failed biometric attempt.
- The alternative method provides reduced functionality compared to biometric methods.
- The alternative method is not consistently available or is difficult to discover.


### Passing techniques
The following techniques are sufficient to meet the requirement:

- Providing at least one fully functional non-biometric alternative for identification or control.
- Ensuring the alternative method is available independently of biometric enrollment.
- Allowing users to complete all tasks using non-biometric methods such as passwords, PINs, passcodes, tokens, or physical credentials.
- Ensuring biometric and non-biometric methods provide equivalent access to ICT functionality without functional restriction.


### Testing methods
To evaluate conformance:

1. Identify all functions where biometric characteristics are used for identification or control.
2. Verify that a non-biometric alternative exists for each function.
3. Confirm that the alternative method can be used without biometric enrollment.
4. Test completion of tasks using only the non-biometric method.
5. Verify that the alternative method provides equivalent outcomes and access.
6. Test under conditions where biometric input fails or is unavailable to ensure access does not depend on successful biometric use.


## Key Terms
**Biometric characteristic:**
A measurable physical or behavioral trait used for identifying or verifying a person (e.g., fingerprint, face geometry, iris pattern, voice).

**User identification:**
The process of verifying or confirming a user's identity before granting access to ICT functionality.

**Control of ICT:**
The use of input methods to operate or interact with ICT systems.

**Non-biometric alternative:**
An authentication or control method that does not use biometric characteristics, such as PINs, passwords, tokens, or physical credentials.

**Biometric authentication:**
The process of verifying a user’s identity by comparing their biometric characteristics against stored reference data using automated systems.
