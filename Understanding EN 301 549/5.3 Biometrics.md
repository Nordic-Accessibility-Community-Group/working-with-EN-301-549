# Understanding 5.3 Biometrics
Status of this document: 

⚠️ Draft mode.

## EN 301 549 v4.1.0 draft
Normative requirement (EN 301 549 v4.1.0 draft)
> “Where ICT uses biological characteristics, the ICT shall not rely on the use of a biological characteristic as the only means of user identification or for control of the ICT.
> NOTE: Some examples of biological characteristics are fingerprints, eye retinal patterns, voice, and face.”

## Intent
Not all users are able to provide or reliably use biological characteristics for identification or interaction with ICT systems. Biometric systems may also fail under certain environmental, technical, or personal conditions.

This requirement exists so that **ICT systems do not create barriers or exclusion by depending exclusively on biological biometrics for access or control**. Users must always be provided with at least one non-biometric alternative method that does not rely on biological characteristics.

Biometric systems can create barriers and exclusion when:
- A user cannot provide the required biological input (e.g., missing fingerprint, facial differences, speech impairment).
- Environmental conditions prevent reliable capture (e.g., low lighting, background noise, gloves, masks).
- Technical limitations cause recognition failures (e.g., sensor accuracy issues or algorithm bias).
- Temporary conditions (injury, illness, voice loss) prevent use of the biometric method.
- Users are excluded due to low accuracy for certain populations (e.g., face recognition bias), or due to variability in biometric traits across individuals.

This requirement ensures **redundancy and inclusivity in ICT systems so they remain available even when biological biometric access and control methods are unavailable, unreliable, or unsuitable for the user**. 


### This requirement benefits
This requirement benefits:

- Users with permanent disabilities affecting biometric capture (e.g., limb differences affecting fingerprint recognition, speech differences affecting voice recognition, facial differences affecting face recognition).
- Users with temporary impairments (e.g., injury, surgery, illness, voice loss).
- Older adults whose biometric traits may change over time.
- Users in environments where biometric capture is unreliable (e.g., poor lighting, noise, wet or dry hands, use of gloves or masks, holding a bag of groceries).
- Users who choose not to use biometric authentication for privacy or personal reasons.


### Examples
If a user has previously set up identification or control with biological characteristics (e.g., fingerprints, face recognition) and later have issues with these biological characteristics (e.g., damaged fingerprint, change of hairstyle impacting facial appearance), user may need a non-biometric way to do log in.

- A smartphone that supports Face ID or fingerprint login must also allow PIN, password, or passcode login.
- A banking application using voice recognition must also support authentication methods that do not rely on voice, such as password and multi-factor authentication.
- A workplace access system using iris scanning must also provide alternatives such as access cards (badge) or PIN-based alternative (passcode or secure tokens).
- A voice-controlled smart device must also provide an alternative input method such as physical buttons or a remote control.


## Evaluation methods
If ICT functionality uses biological characteristics, verify that:
- A non-biometric alternative method exists for all functions requiring biometric input.
- The alternative method does not require prior successful biometric enrollment.
- The alternative provides equivalent access to the same functionality.
- The alternative is available in all contexts where the biometric method is available.


### Failures
The following are considered failures:
- The ICT relies solely on biological characteristics for authentication or control.
- No alternative method is available that does not rely on biometric input.
- The alternative method requires prior biometric enrollment before it can be used.
- The alternative method is limited to fallback or error conditions only.
- The alternative method provides reduced functionality compared to the biometric method.
- The alternative method is difficult to discover or not consistently available.


### Passing techniques
The following techniques are sufficient to meet the requirement:
- Providing at least one fully functional non-biometric alternative for identification or control.
- Ensuring the alternative method is available independently of biometric enrollment.
- Allowing users to complete the same tasks using non-biometric methods such as passwords, PINs, passcodes, tokens, or physical credentials.
- Ensuring biometric and non-biometric methods provide equivalent access to ICT functionality.


### Testing methods
To evaluate conformance:
1. Identify all functions where biological characteristics are used for identification or control.
2. Verify that a non-biometric alternative exists for each function.
3. Confirm that the alternative method can be used without enrolling or enabling biometric features.
4. Test completion of tasks using only the non-biometric method.
5. Verify that the alternative method provides equivalent outcomes and access.
6. Test under conditions where biometric input fails or is unavailable to ensure fallback independence is not required for access.


## Key Terms
**Biological characteristic (biometric):**
A measurable physical or behavioral trait used for identifying or verifying a person (e.g., fingerprint, face geometry, iris pattern, voice).

**User identification:**
The process of verifying or confirming a user's identity before granting access to ICT functionality.

**Control of ICT:**
The use of input methods to operate, navigate, or interact with ICT systems or functions.

**Non-biometric alternative:**
An authentication or control method that does not rely on biological characteristics, such as PINs, passwords, tokens, or physical credentials.
