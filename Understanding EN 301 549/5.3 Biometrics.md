# Understanding 5.3 Biometrics
Status of this document: **⚠️ Draft mode.**

## In Brief
### Goal
No specific biological features are required for logging into or controlling ICT

### What to do
Give users an alternative to using biometrics

### Why it is important
People can use ICT no matter which biological features they have


## Requirement from EN 301 549 v4.1.0 draft
> Where ICT uses biological characteristics,
> the ICT shall not rely on the use of a biological characteristic as the only means of user identification or for control of the ICT.
>
> NOTE: Some examples of biological characteristics are fingerprints, eye retinal patterns, voice, and face.


## Intent
Not all users are able to provide or reliably use biometric characteristics for identification or interaction with ICT systems. Biometric systems may also fail under environmental, technical, or user-related conditions.

This requirement ensures that biometric characteristics are not the sole means of user identification or control of ICT systems and requires that **at least one non-biometric alternative is always available**.

> Editorial note: Where the wording of the requirement does not make it clear whether it is acceptable to use *another* biolical characteristic in an alternative, Annex C of the standard specifies "another means that does not rely on a biological characteristic". The interpretation of this Understanding Document uses this definition, even though Annex C is not normative. 

Biometric systems can create **barriers to access** when:

- Conditions prevent users from providing biometric input (e.g., missing fingerprint, facial differences, speech impairment)
- Environmental conditions prevent reliable capture (e.g., low light, background noise, gloves, masks)
- Technical limitations prevent accurate recognition (e.g., sensor errors or algorithmic bias in biometric recognition systems)
- User conditions prevent use (e.g., injury, illness, voice loss, carrying an object, temporary constraints)
- Systemic variability or bias leads to exclusion (e.g., demographic variation in recognition accuracy)


### Scope
This clause applies to ICT systems and services that use biometric characteristics for:

- User identification or authentication (e.g., login, sign-in, account access)
- Control of ICT functionality (e.g., voice commands, gesture interaction, biometric unlocking of features or devices)

This requirement applies to both access to ICT systems and the control of ICT functions after access has been granted.

It applies regardless of:

- Whether biometric use is primary or secondary in the system design
- Whether biometrics are used alone or as part of multi-factor authentication
- Whether biometric processing occurs locally or remotely
- Whether biometric methods are optional, default, or user-enabled


### Non-biometric alternative
The requirement states that at least one alternative method that does not rely on biometric characteristics shall be provided for all functionality that requires biometric characteristics for identification or control.

The alternative method shall:

- Be available without requiring successful biometric enrollment
- Provide access to the same functionality as the biometric method
- Not depend on successful biometric recognition
- Not require the availability of biometric input to function
- Provide equivalent access to the ICT functionality


## Benefits
This requirement benefits:

- Users with permanent disabilities affecting biometric capture (e.g., limb differences affecting fingerprint recognition, speech differences affecting voice recognition).
- Users with temporary impairments (e.g., injury, surgery, illness, voice loss).
- Users whose biometric characteristics change over time.
- Users in environments where biometric capture is unreliable (e.g., low-light conditions, noise, wet or dry hands, gloves, masks, or carrying objects).
- Users who choose not to use biometric authentication for privacy reasons.


## Examples
- A user who cannot use fingerprint authentication can use a password or PIN.
- A mobile device that supports facial recognition also supports passcode authentication.
- A voice authentication system also supports password-based or multi-factor authentication methods.
- An access control system using iris scanning also supports access cards (badges), PIN-based authentication, or physical tokens (passcodes, secure tokens).
- If a user has previously enrolled in biometric authentication and later cannot use those biometric characteristics (e.g., due to injury or changes affecting recognition), they can still access the ICT using a non-biometric method.

## Techniques

> ## Evaluation methods

> If ICT functionality uses biometric characteristics, verify that:

> - A non-biometric alternative method is available for all functions that use biometric characteristics.
> - The alternative method does not depend on prior biometric enrollment.
> - The alternative provides equivalent functionality to biometric methods.
> - The alternative is available under the same conditions as biometric characteristics.


> ### Testing methods

> To validate conformance in practice:

> - Identify all functions that use biometric characteristics.
> - Verify that a non-biometric alternative exists for each function.
> - Verify that the alternative can be used without biometric enrollment.
> - Execute end-to-end task completion using only the non-biometric method.
> - Verify that equivalent functionality is achieved without biometric use.


### Failures
The following are considered failures:

- The ICT depends solely on biometric characteristics for identification or control and no non-biometric alternative method is available.
- The alternative method requires prior biometric enrollment.
- The alternative method is only available after biometric failure.
- The alternative method does not provide equivalent functionality.
- The alternative method is not reliably available or discoverable.
- The alternative method is not available for users who have originally enrolled using biometrics


### Suffient techniques

> Editorial note: These are not mutually exclusive ways of meeting the requirement, as Suffucient Techniques are supposed to be, but rather a list of requirements that all apply at the same time

The following techniques are sufficient to meet the requirement:

- Providing at least one non-biometric alternative method for all biometric functionality.
- Ensuring the alternative is available independently of biometric enrollment.
- Allowing full task completion using non-biometric methods such as passwords, PINs, tokens, or physical credentials.
- Ensuring equivalent functionality between biometric and non-biometric methods.


## Key Terms
**Biometric authentication:**
The process of verifying a user's identity by comparing biometric characteristics against stored reference data.

**Biometric characteristic:**
A measurable physical or behavioral trait used for identification or verification (e.g., fingerprint, face geometry, iris pattern, voice).

> Editorial note: The requirement talks about "biological characteristics", but this is not on the terms list?

**Biometric enrollment:** The process of capturing, registering, and storing a user’s biometric characteristics for use in subsequent biometric methods for identification or verification.

**Biometric method:** A method of identifying or verifying a user based on biometric characteristics, typically involving automated comparison of captured biometric data with stored reference data.

**Control of ICT:**
The use of input methods to operate or interact with ICT systems and functions.

**Non-biometric alternative:**
An authentication or control method that does not use biometric characteristics, such as PINs, passwords, tokens, or physical credentials.

**User identification:**
The process of verifying or confirming a user's identity before granting access to ICT functionality.
