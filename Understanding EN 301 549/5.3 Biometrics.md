# Understanding 5.3 Biometrics
Status of this document: **⚠️ Draft mode.**

## In Brief
### Goal
People can identify themselves or control ICT without having to use a biological characteristic.

### What to do
Provide another means of user identification or ICT control that does not rely on a biological characteristic.

### Why it is important
Some people cannot provide, enroll, or reliably use a specific biological characteristic. Others may be temporarily unable to use it, or the biometric capture may fail because of environmental or technical conditions.

## Requirement from Draft EN 301 549 V4.1.0
> Where ICT uses biological characteristics,
> the ICT shall not rely on the use of a biological characteristic as the only means of user identification or for control of the ICT.
>
> NOTE: Some examples of biological characteristics are fingerprints, eye retinal patterns, voice, and face.

## Intent
The requirement prevents a biological characteristic from becoming the only way to identify a user or control ICT.

When ICT uses biometrics for these purposes, users must have another way to complete the same identification or control task. The alternative must not depend on successful biometric enrollment, capture, or recognition.

This matters because biometric systems can exclude users when:

- the relevant biological characteristic is missing, changed, injured, or difficult to capture
- the user has a speech, facial, limb, or other difference that affects recognition
- lighting, noise, gloves, masks, wet hands, or similar conditions interfere with capture
- a sensor or recognition system fails

## Scope
This clause applies when ICT uses a biological characteristic for:

- user identification, such as sign-in, account access, or identity verification
- control of ICT, such as unlocking functionality or issuing commands

The clause is not limited to mandatory biometric features. If a biometric method is offered for user identification or ICT control, there must be a non-biometric way to perform that identification or control task.

The clause does not require every possible method to be non-biometric. It requires that the biological characteristic is not the only available means.

## Examples
- A phone that supports face recognition also allows sign-in with a passcode.
- A laptop that supports fingerprint sign-in also allows sign-in with a password, PIN, or security key.
- A voice authentication service also supports an authentication method that does not require voice.
- A system that uses iris scanning for access also supports a badge, PIN, or token.
- A voice-controlled function also provides a non-voice control method.

## Evaluation Methods
Annex C.5.3 gives the assessment type as testing.

### Preconditions
The requirement applies when the ICT uses a biological characteristic for user identification or for control of ICT.

If the ICT does not use a biological characteristic for either purpose, this clause is not applicable.

### Procedure
For each user identification or ICT control task that uses a biological characteristic:

- identify the biometric method and the task it supports
- check that another means can be used for the same task
- check that the other means does not rely on a biological characteristic
- check that the other means can be used without successful biometric enrollment, capture, or recognition

### Pass
The ICT passes when another means that does not rely on a biological characteristic can be used for the relevant user identification or ICT control task.

### Fail
The ICT fails when the biological characteristic is the only means available for the relevant user identification or ICT control task.

Common failures include:

- biometric enrollment is required before the user can use the alternative
- the alternative appears only after the user attempts and fails biometric recognition
- the alternative identifies the user but does not give access to the same identification or control outcome
- one biological characteristic is replaced only by another biological characteristic

## Benefits
This requirement benefits:

- users who cannot provide a requested biological characteristic
- users whose biometric characteristics change over time
- users with temporary injuries, illness, or voice loss
- users in environments where biometric capture is unreliable
- users affected by sensor or recognition-system limitations

## Key Terms
**Biological characteristic:** A physical or behavioral characteristic used by ICT for user identification or control. The standard gives fingerprints, eye retinal patterns, voice, and face as examples.

**Biometric method:** A method that uses a biological characteristic for user identification or control of ICT.

**Non-biometric alternative:** A method for the same identification or control task that does not rely on a biological characteristic, such as a password, PIN, badge, token, or security key.

**User identification:** A process used to identify, verify, or authenticate a user before granting access or performing an action.

**Control of ICT:** Operation of ICT functionality, such as unlocking, issuing commands, or controlling features.
