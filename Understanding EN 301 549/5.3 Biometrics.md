# Understanding 5.3 Biometrics
Status of this document: **⚠️ Draft mode.**

This page is the Nordic Accessibility Community Group's understanding of Draft EN 301 549 V4.1.0 clause 5.3. It is meant to help readers discuss and apply the clause; it is not part of EN 301 549.

## In Brief
### Goal
People can identify themselves or control ICT without having to use a biological characteristic.

### What to do
Provide another means of user identification or ICT control that does not rely on a biological characteristic.

### Why it matters
Some people cannot provide, set up, or reliably use specific biological characteristics. Others may be temporarily unable to use them, or the biometric capture may fail because of environmental or technical conditions.

## Requirement from Draft EN 301 549 V4.1.0
> Where ICT uses biological characteristics,
> the ICT shall not rely on the use of a biological characteristic as the only means of user identification or for control of the ICT.
>
> NOTE: Some examples of biological characteristics are fingerprints, eye retinal patterns, voice, and face.

## Intent
The requirement prevents biological characteristics from becoming the only way to identify a user or control ICT.

When ICT uses biometrics for identification or control, users need another way to perform the same task. When checking the alternative, consider whether users can reach and use it without first setting up or using the biometric method.

Biometric systems can block users when:

- the relevant biological characteristic is missing, changed, injured, or difficult to capture
- lighting, noise, gloves, masks, wet hands, or similar conditions interfere with capture
- a sensor or recognition system fails

## Scope
This clause applies when ICT uses a biological characteristic for:

- user identification, such as sign-in, account access, or identity verification
- control of ICT, such as unlocking features or issuing commands

Using a body part or a spoken command is not enough by itself to bring a feature into this clause. For this clause, the ICT has to use a biological characteristic of the person. Tapping a touchscreen, using a camera for a gesture, or speaking a command that any speaker could give is different from using a fingerprint, face, eye retinal pattern, or a person's voice as a biological characteristic to identify the user or control an action.

The clause is not limited to mandatory biometric features. If a biometric method is offered for user identification or ICT control, users need a non-biometric way to perform that identification or control task.

The clause does not require every possible method to be non-biometric. It requires that the biological characteristic is not the only available means.

The non-biometric alternative may still need to meet other applicable EN 301 549 requirements.

## Examples
- A phone that supports face recognition also allows sign-in with a passcode.
- A laptop that supports fingerprint sign-in also allows sign-in with a password, PIN, or security key.
- A user who has already set up fingerprint sign-in can still sign in with a non-biometric method if the fingerprint cannot be used later.
- A voice authentication service also supports an authentication method that does not require voice.
- A system that uses iris scanning for access also supports a badge, PIN, or token.
- A system that uses voice recognition to identify or authenticate the speaker also provides a non-voice method for the same task.

## Evaluation Methods
Annex C.5.3 gives the assessment type as testing.

### Preconditions
The requirement applies when the ICT uses a biological characteristic for user identification or for control of ICT.

If the ICT does not use a biological characteristic for either purpose, this clause is not applicable.

### Procedure
For each user identification or ICT control task that uses a biological characteristic:

- identify the biometric method and the task it supports
- check that another means can be used for the same task
- check that the other means does not rely on a biological characteristic

### Pass
The ICT passes when another means that does not rely on a biological characteristic can be used for the relevant user identification or ICT control task.

### Fail
The ICT fails when the biological characteristic is the only means available for the relevant user identification or ICT control task.

Common failures include:

- the user has to set up a fingerprint, face, voice, or other biological characteristic before they can use the alternative
- the alternative identifies the user but does not give access to the same identification or control outcome
- one biological characteristic is replaced only by another biological characteristic

This case may be a failure:

- the alternative appears only after the user attempts and fails biometric recognition. Check whether users who cannot provide the biological characteristic can still reach and use the alternative.

## Benefits
People who benefit include:

- users who cannot provide a requested biological characteristic
- users whose biometric characteristics change over time
- users with temporary injuries, illness, or voice loss
- users in environments where biometric capture is unreliable
- users whose biometric input is not reliably captured or recognized

## Key Terms
**Biological characteristic:** A characteristic of a person used by ICT for user identification or control. The standard gives fingerprints, eye retinal patterns, voice, and face as examples.

**Biometric method:** A method that uses a biological characteristic for user identification or control of ICT.

**Non-biometric alternative:** A method for the same identification or control task that does not rely on a biological characteristic, such as a password, PIN, badge, token, or security key.

**User identification:** A process used to identify, verify, or authenticate a user before granting access or performing an action.

**Control of ICT:** Operation of ICT features, such as unlocking, issuing commands, or changing settings.

## Interpretation Notes
In this Understanding document, 5.3 is applied as follows: if ICT uses a biological characteristic to identify a user or control ICT, there must also be another way to identify the user or control the ICT without using biological characteristics.

The reason is Annex C. In Draft EN 301 549 V4.1.0, Annex C is normative. C.5.3 says to check that another means can be used for user identification or control of ICT, and that this other means does not rely on a biological characteristic. A second biometric method, such as face recognition instead of fingerprint recognition, would still rely on a biological characteristic.

This is different from EN 301 549 V3.2.1. That version said ICT shall not rely on "a particular biological characteristic" as the only means. Its notes said the alternative could be biometric or non-biometric. The V4.1.0 draft removes "particular" and removes those notes.

The title of 5.3 is "Biometrics", while the requirement text says "biological characteristic". The draft does not define that term in clause 5.3. This document uses it for the kinds of biometric features listed in the clause: fingerprints, eye retinal patterns, voice, and face.

The clause also uses the phrase "control of ICT". This is about control that depends on a biological characteristic. It does not include ordinary touch, spoken commands, or gestures. For example, a spoken command is not biometric control just because it uses speech. It becomes relevant to 5.3 when the ICT uses the person's voice as a biological characteristic, such as to recognize who is speaking or approve an action.
